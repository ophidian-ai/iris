# Local Business Research

Research local businesses to find potential clients with outdated or missing websites.

## When to Use

When Eric wants to find small businesses in a specific area that could benefit from a new or updated website.

## Inputs

- **Location** (city, town, or area to search)
- **Industry/niche** (optional -- e.g., restaurants, salons, trades, retail)
- **Radius or scope** (optional -- e.g., "within 30 miles", "downtown area")

## Process

1. Search for small businesses in the specified area and industry
2. Check their current web presence (website, social media, Google Business listing)
3. Evaluate their website quality if one exists (mobile-friendly, modern design, load speed, SEO basics)
4. Identify specific opportunities (no website, outdated design, not mobile-friendly, poor SEO, missing contact info, etc.)

## Output Format

A table with the following columns:

| Business Name | Location | Industry | Website | Issues Found | Opportunity | Contact Info |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Example Bakery | Springfield, OH | Bakery | www.example.com | Not mobile-friendly, slow | Redesign | (555) 123-4567 |
| Joe's Plumbing | Springfield, OH | Trades | None | No web presence | New site | Found on Google Maps |

After the table, include:

- **Top 3 prospects** -- The businesses with the clearest need and easiest pitch
- **Suggested outreach approach** for each (what to mention in the cold email)

## Rules

- Focus on businesses that are clearly operational but have a weak web presence.
- Prioritize businesses that appear to have revenue (established, reviews, active) but a website that doesn't match their quality.
- Don't include businesses with already-modern, well-built websites.
- Note the source of contact information (Google Maps, Yelp, Facebook, etc.).
- If Eric specifies an industry, focus there. If not, cast a wide net across common local business types.
- Be honest about what you can and can't verify. Flag assumptions.
